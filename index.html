A Better Understanding of SDS

Score distillation sampling1 enables the use of diffusion priors for tasks operating in data poor domains. However, it suffers from signficant artifacts that a number of follow-up works have tried to address2 3 4 5 6. Here, we show how SDS is used to optimize a 3D NeRF using a text-to-image model.

We show that SDS (and its variants) can be cast as a Schrödinger Bridge (SB) problem, which aims to find the optimal transport between two distributions. With SDS, these optimal paths connect the current optimized image distribution (e.g., renderings from a NeRF) to a target distribution (e.g., text-conditioned natural image distribution).

A Schrödinger Bridge transports between corresponding samples in the source and target distributions.
SDS is best understood as sampling SB paths, subject to approximation error.
SDS methods and their variants can be well understood as per-iteration estimates of the Schrödinger Bridge. These estimates are subject to the following two sources of errors:
First-Order Approximation Error

Instead of training a model to solve this problem directly, we use pre-trained diffusion models to approximate the bridge. This requires solving two PF-ODEs, which invoke dozens of function evaluations (NFEs) to estimate the gradient each iteration. Instead it is practical to use single step estimates, which are less accurate and can induce errors. Recent works ISM [2] and SDI [3] can be interpreted as reducing this error with a multi-step simulation.
Source Distribution Mismatch

Estimating the Schrödinger Bridge relies on \(\mathrm{\epsilon}_{\phi, \text{src}}\) accurately estimating the distribution of the current sample, \(x_{\theta}\). A series of works can be viewed as improving this error [4, 5, 6]. SDS (under high CFG) uses the uncondtional distribution as a proxy for the current distribution, contributing to its characteristic artifacts.


We show that this unified framework explains VSD's high quality results and the shortcomings of other, more efficient methods. Please read our paper for a thorough analysis.



Our Simple Improvement

Our insight is that pre-trained diffusion models have learned the distribution of natural and corrupted images through a combination of powerful text representation and enormous image-caption datasets. We find that by simply describing image corruptions with a text prompt, we can improve our estimate of the source distribution.

To describe the artifacts produced by SDS, we append standard negative descriptors and drop the descriptors of the high-quality generation. That's it. We find that the specifc descriptors are not too important and many variations work well.
